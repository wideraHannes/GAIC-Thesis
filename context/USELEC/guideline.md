A sentence should be labeled as an argument if it plays a persuasive role by asserting a claim or providing a reason for or against a claim. Claims are statements that express a stance, opinion, policy position, judgment, or conclusion that is not simply an indisputable fact and would reasonably require justification, such as “I favor higher salaries for teachers” or “The present tax structure is a disgrace to this country.” Premises are also argumentative and consist of reasons, evidence, examples, statistics, or quotations that support or attack a claim, for instance citing job growth numbers to justify that an administration performed better economically. Claims and premises may appear with explicit indicators like “I believe,” “so,” “because,” or “since,” but indicators are neither required nor sufficient on their own; context must show that the statement is being used to argue. A sentence can be an argument even if no supporting premises are given, as often happens in opening or closing debate statements. Conversely, a sentence should be labeled as no-argument if it does not assert a debatable position or function as a justification, such as purely procedural remarks, background information with no argumentative intent, or factual statements presented without being used to support or refute a claim. Each clause should be judged by its role in context, noting that a statement can serve as a claim in one context and as a premise in another, but it cannot be both at the same time.
