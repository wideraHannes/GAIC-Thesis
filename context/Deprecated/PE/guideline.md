A sentence should be marked as an argument if it expresses a debatable position or provides a reason that supports or challenges such a position, rather than merely stating facts or background information. Arguments consist of claims—statements the author wants the reader to accept, such as “human cloning should be prohibited”—and premises, which justify or refute those claims, for example “because its consequences are unpredictable.” Claims typically express opinions, evaluations, or recommendations that are not self-evident and often appear as conclusions in a paragraph, while premises give reasons, evidence, or examples for those claims. Sentences that only describe definitions, historical facts, topic introductions, rhetorical questions, or organizational remarks (e.g., “I will discuss three reasons”) are not arguments. Likewise, recommendations for future action at the very end of an essay, if not presented as reasons within an argument, are treated as non-argumentative. Indicators like “therefore,” “because,” or “however” can signal arguments, but their presence alone is insufficient; the key test is whether the sentence plays a role in persuading the reader by supporting or attacking a standpoint. For instance, “Wearing school uniforms restricts self-determination” functions as an argument when it is used as a reason for or against a policy, whereas “School uniforms are common in many countries” is not argumentative because it only states a fact.
