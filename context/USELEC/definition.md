An argument is any span of text in which a speaker puts forward a standpoint or supports a standpoint on a controversial issue. It consists of claims and premises: a claim expresses a position, opinion, judgment, or advocated policy that the speaker wants the audience to accept, such as taking a stance on an issue, evaluating an opponent, or asserting the effectiveness of an action. A premise is an assertion offered as a reason or justification for a claim, including evidence such as facts, statistics, examples, past experiences, or causal explanations introduced explicitly or implicitly (e.g., through markers like “because”). Arguments may be expressed within a single sentence or, more rarely, across sentence boundaries, and a sentence can contain more than one argumentative component. What is not an argument includes purely descriptive, narrative, or procedural statements that do not advance or support a standpoint, as well as questions, rhetorical fillers, greetings, transitions, or remarks that resemble argumentative language but do not actually state a position or provide support for one. Statements that merely manage the interaction or ask what should be done, without asserting or justifying a viewpoint, are also considered non-argumentative.
