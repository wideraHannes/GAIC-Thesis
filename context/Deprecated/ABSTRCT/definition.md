An argument is a textual component that contributes to evidence-based reasoning by either stating a conclusion about a study’s outcome or providing observations that justify or challenge such conclusions. It consists of claims and evidence: a claim is an author’s concluding statement about the effectiveness, comparison, or outcome of an intervention, derived from the study’s results, while evidence is an observed fact or measurement from the study, such as outcomes, side effects, or statistical results, that can support or undermine another component. Claims may be general or specific and can appear anywhere in the text, not only at the end, and more general claims can be supported by more specific ones. Evidence is treated as credible observations that do not require further justification within the text because they report measured results. An argument is defined not in isolation but through its potential to stand in a support or attack relation with another component. Text that merely provides background information, describes study design or procedures without reporting results, introduces a topic without connecting to study outcomes, or states problems or limitations unconnected to the study’s findings is not considered an argument. Similarly, purely descriptive context or introductory material that does not assert a conclusion or report an observation relevant to supporting or attacking a conclusion is not an argument.
