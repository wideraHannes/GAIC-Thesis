A sentence should be labeled as an argument if it contains at least one explicit argumentative component, meaning a claim or supporting data, rather than being purely descriptive or procedural. Claims are statements the authors put forward for acceptance and can often be paraphrased as what the authors think or believe; for example, “Skeleton Subspace Deformation is the predominant approach to character skinning” counts as a claim because it makes a debatable assessment. Claims may concern general background beliefs in the field or specific assertions about the authors’ own work, often signaled by phrases like “we suggest,” “we argue,” or “we claim,” though such cues are not strictly required. Data are facts, observations, experimental results, examples, figures, tables, or citations that provide evidence or preconditions supporting a claim, such as references to prior studies or conditions introduced by “if” or “when.” A sentence can include multiple argumentative components, and only the minimal text span that expresses the claim or data should be annotated, excluding connectors like “because” or “however.” In contrast, a sentence should be labeled as no-argument if it merely defines terms, explains notation, describes methods or implementation steps, announces upcoming sections without stating properties, or provides conventions, such as “Let us denote matrices by capital letters.” Similarly, announcements of intent without substantive assertions, or straightforward descriptions that do not advance or support a position, are considered non-argumentative.
