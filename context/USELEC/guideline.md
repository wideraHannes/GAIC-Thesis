Annotators label a sentence or clause as argumentative if it asserts a claim that takes a stance, opinion, policy position, or judgment that could reasonably be supported or attacked, regardless of whether support is explicitly given. Use practical cues such as opinion markers (e.g., "I believe," "in my judgment," "I think," "I am in favor of"), conclusion markers ("so," "therefore," "for this reason"), or contrastive cues ("however"), but indicators alone never guarantee an argument—context must show an asserted position. Purely factual statements that are certainly true or false are non-argumentative unless they function as reasons for or against a claim; the same factual content becomes argumentative when used as justification. Claims may appear at the beginning or end of a turn, may answer a moderator’s question, and may occur without any indicators, especially in oral debate, so annotators must infer argumentative intent from the surrounding discourse. If a statement is given as a reason for another statement, label it as a premise; however, if that reason itself expresses a controversial assertion needing justification, still label it as a claim (nested arguments). A clause cannot be both claim and premise; decide its role relative to the immediate context. Boundary examples: (a) "I favor higher salaries for teachers" → Claim, because it states a policy stance needing justification. (b) "There was a total growth of nineteen percent over seven years" → Premise, because it supplies evidence for a comparative claim. (c) "Communism is the enemy of all religions" → Claim, even when used as a reason, because it is itself a debatable assertion. Segment at clause level when multiple independent argumentative units occur in one sentence.
