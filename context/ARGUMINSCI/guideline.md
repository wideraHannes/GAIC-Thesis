Annotators mark a sentence (or clause) as argumentative if it expresses a claim or provides data supporting or contradicting a claim; purely descriptive, definitional, procedural, or mathematical explanations are non-argumentative. Claims are identified by assessive or opinionated statements that could be disputed or supported, often signaled by phrases like "we think," "we suggest," "we argue," or evaluative wording; they can be implicit and paraphrasable as "we think that…" even without explicit cues. Distinguish claims from data by asking whether the span states a debatable assertion (claim) versus an established fact, observation, citation, example, figure, table, or precondition (data). Proposals or announcements without asserted properties are not argumentative (e.g., Example 5: "we propose a unified framework…" → non-argumentative because no claim about the framework is made). Background assessments presented as general facts are still claims if they reflect a community-level judgment (Example 11: "SSD is the predominant approach…" → background claim). Announced future reasoning or demonstrations are not data (Example 3: "To demonstrate our approach…" → non-argumentative), while conditional or example clauses introduced by cues like "if," "for," or "e.g." are annotated as data, excluding the cue phrase. Boundary cases: Example 8 ("Let us denote matrices…") → non-argumentative because it only sets notation; Example 2 background sentence without explicit markers → background claim because it can be paraphrased as an author belief and is supported by citations; Example 6 evaluative self-assessment → own claims, with contrast cues like "however" signaling contradictions.
