Label a tweet/sentence as Argument if it contains at least one claim (even implicit) that asserts a position, evaluation, policy, or judgment that could be supported or attacked; otherwise label No-Argument. Enforce dataset-specific exclusions: if necessary context is missing (e.g., refers to an unavailable parent tweet) or the text is not in English, label No-Argument. For multi-sentence tweets, any single claim makes the whole tweet Argument. Treat questions as Argument when they implicitly advance a claim or reasoning (e.g., “If Middle-earth is the whole ‘Earth,’ then why does the map extend…?”), and treat implicit prompts as claims when they clearly imply a stance/request for evidence (e.g., “Show me the descriptions of skin colors in Tolkien’s writing” → Argument). Do not count pure insults as claims (e.g., “don’t be a dick about it” → No-Argument). Use cue phrases as signals but not guarantees: claim indicators include “I believe,” “I think,” “in my judgment/opinion,” “I am in favor of,” “however,” “so,” “for this reason,” while premise indicators include “because,” “since,” and “for example”; still rely on context because indicators can appear without an actual claim. Boundary/nested case rule: a statement used as a reason may still be annotated as a claim if it is itself a disputable assertion needing justification (e.g., “Communism is the enemy of all religions” → claim/Argument even when functioning as support), and clauses are segmented by meaning (not punctuation), with no clause labeled as both claim and premise.
