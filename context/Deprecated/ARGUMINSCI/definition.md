An argument consists of explicit argumentative components that advance or evaluate propositions in scientific writing, namely claims and data. Claims are statements that assert something as true, either about the authors’ own contributions or about background knowledge, prior work, or common practices in the field. Data are factual statements, examples, references, or observations that serve as evidence for or against a claim, and claims themselves may also support or contradict other claims. These components can be linked by relations such as support, contradiction, or semantic equivalence, forming a structured network of reasoning, and they may span from a few words to multiple sentences. Content that does not assert a proposition or provide evidence—such as purely procedural descriptions, neutral narrative text, definitions without argumentative intent, or general exposition lacking a claim–evidence role—is not considered an argument. Likewise, rhetorical elements that do not function as claims or data, including implicit warrants or qualifiers not explicitly stated, fall outside the scope of what counts as an argument.
