A sentence should be labeled as an Argument if it plays a role in the study’s reasoning by making a claim or providing evidence for or against a claim. Claims are author interpretations or conclusions, such as general statements comparing treatments, asserting effectiveness, safety, non-inferiority, or suggesting clinical implications; for example, a sentence stating that a treatment was more effective than another or that results support its use counts as an argument. Premises are also arguments and consist of factual observations or measurements from the study, including numerical outcomes, statistical results, side effects, or even explicitly negative findings (e.g., no significant difference), because they serve as evidence. Typical signals of claims include phrases like “these results suggest,” “according to the results,” or broad comparative or evaluative language rather than raw data. In contrast, a sentence should be labeled No-Argument if it merely describes background information, study aims, methods, definitions, trial design, patient characteristics, procedural details, or terminology without asserting or supporting a conclusion. Purely underspecified or vague statements that do not clearly assert a general conclusion (such as noting that fewer side effects occurred without interpretation) are also not arguments. Overall, the key distinction is whether the sentence contributes to justifying, attacking, or concluding a standpoint, as opposed to simply reporting context or setup.
