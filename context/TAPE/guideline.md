Label a tweet/sentence as Argument if it contains at least one claim (explicit or implied), including major-claim-like stance statements; in multi-sentence tweets, the presence of any single claim makes the whole tweet Argument. Label No-Argument if required context is missing (e.g., it refers to an unavailable parent tweet) or if the text is not in English. Treat implicit claims (requests or prompts that presuppose a disputable point) as valid claims (e.g., “Show me the descriptions of skin colors in Tolkien’s writing” = Argument). Do not treat pure insults as claims; if the content is only insulting with no propositional assertion, label No-Argument (e.g., “don’t be a dick about it” = No-Argument). Questions are generally non-argumentative unless they function as/contain a claim; rhetorical questions can introduce a topic but are not claims by themselves. Use cue phrases to spot argumentative content: conclusion/claim signals like “therefore/thus/as a result/consequently/so” and premise signals like “because/since/for example/in addition,” but do not rely on them blindly (they can mark intermediate steps). Boundary examples from the guidelines: Example 2.4.6 “Basically I think human cloning is against the laws of nature” = Argument, while the preceding rhetorical question “If human cloning became possible what would be the outcome of it?” = No-Argument; Example 2.1.3 “More advance sport lessons should be provided…, since …” = Argument (has a claim); Example 2.1.4 “I do not agree with the opponents of nuclear power” = Argument because the negated stance is content-bearing (not mere shell language).
